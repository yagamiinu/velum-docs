A privacy layer you can't trust is worse than none. So we'd rather tell you exactly where Velum stands than overclaim.

## Current status

<Note>
  **Velum is live on testnet.** It has not completed a third-party security audit
  or a production trusted-setup ceremony. Do not use it to protect real value
  until both are done and published here.
</Note>

## Pre-mainnet milestones

<Steps>
  <Step title="Security audit">
    Independent review of the pool contracts, the Groth16 verifier, the circuits,
    and the relayer. Findings and remediations will be published.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Trusted-setup ceremony">
    Groth16 requires a per-circuit trusted setup. A production ceremony with
    multiple independent participants replaces the development parameters used on
    testnet. As long as one participant is honest and discards their toxic waste,
    the setup is secure. The transcript will be public.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Mainnet deployment">
    Only after the above, with published addresses and a release manifest.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Why the trusted setup matters

Groth16 is fast and cheap to verify on-chain — which is why Velum uses it — but its setup produces "toxic waste" that, if retained by an attacker, could let them forge proofs (and thus mint withdrawals). A multi-party ceremony reduces that risk to "at least one participant was honest." Until the production ceremony is complete, **testnet parameters must be treated as compromised by construction.**

## Responsible disclosure

Found something? Report it privately before disclosing publicly. Security contact details will be published alongside the audit. Do not test against real funds, and do not disclose a vulnerability in a way that puts users at risk.

## What we won't cut corners on

- No mainnet before audit **and** ceremony.
- No custody, no master keys, no backdoors — verified in the audited code.
- Public transcripts, public addresses, public verifier. If you can't verify it, don't trust it.
