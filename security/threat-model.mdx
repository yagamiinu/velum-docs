Security is only meaningful relative to an adversary. This page states who Velum defends you against, what it assumes, and where the limits are.

## Adversary: a global chain observer

Assume an adversary who sees **every block on every chain**, indefinitely, and runs the best available de-anonymization tooling. This is the realistic adversary for public ledgers.

**Velum defends against** this observer linking your deposit to your withdrawal — *provided* you use a relayer, a fresh recipient, and a healthy anonymity set. The zero-knowledge proof reveals only that some valid note was spent.

**Velum does not defend against** you re-establishing the link yourself: reusing wallets, funding a "fresh" address from your main one, withdrawing a rare exact amount, or withdrawing seconds after depositing. See [The privacy model](/introduction/privacy-model).

## Trust assumptions

| Component | Assumption |
| --- | --- |
| Circuit + verifier | Sound Groth16 setup (post-ceremony). Toxic waste discarded. |
| Merkle tree / pool | Correct contract, verified on-chain, UUPS owner = honest multisig. |
| Relayer | Untrusted for integrity (can't alter your withdrawal — it's bound in the proof), semi-trusted for liveness (can refuse to submit). |
| Sanctions oracle | Maintained against current lists; a stale oracle weakens screening, not fund safety. |
| Your device | Trusted. Secrets are generated and proofs computed locally; a compromised device compromises your notes. |

## What a malicious relayer can and cannot do

- **Cannot** change the recipient, amount, or fee — these are public inputs bound into the proof.
- **Cannot** learn your secrets or link your deposit — it only sees a finished proof.
- **Can** refuse to submit (denial of service). Mitigation: multiple independent relayers, or self-submit as a fallback.

## What a malicious pool owner can and cannot do

- **Cannot** forge proofs or seize shielded funds under the intended design.
- **Can**, via the UUPS upgrade path, change contract logic. Mitigation: owner is a multisig, upgrades are observable on-chain, and this is a focus of the audit. Watch the upgrade key.

## Current design properties to understand

- **Amounts are visible.** In the current design the withdrawn amount is a public value, so amount-matching is a de-anonymization vector. Encrypted-note amounts (below) close this — until then, prefer round, common amounts and split large ones.
- **Privacy scales with the anonymity set.** Privacy is statistical: the more deposits between yours and your withdrawal, the stronger it is. A sparse pool offers weak privacy regardless of the cryptography.

## Roadmap items that strengthen the model

- **Encrypted-note amounts** (hide the withdrawn amount).
- **Persistent shielded balances + shield→shield transfers** (keep value private end-to-end instead of exiting to a public address).
- **Deterministic key derivation** (remove the "save your Access Key or lose funds" failure mode).
- **Growing, cross-chain anonymity sets** via the omnichain design.
